Psychoeducation: Neural Pathways & Boundary Formation

```

Part 1: How Neural Pathways Work

"Let's talk about what's happening in your brain when you practice new thoughts, because this ties directly into boundaries. Your brain has spent years building superhighways—automatic pathways that go: discomfort → negative thought → 'I'm a drug addict' → craving → use. That highway is strong because you've driven it thousands of times. It's the path of least resistance."

"What you did yesterday? You got off that highway. You took a dirt path—the one that goes: discomfort → notice the thought → call for support → reframe → choose differently. Right now, that dirt path is bumpy and slow. But every single time you take it, you're paving it. You're making it smoother, faster, more automatic. Eventually, that becomes your new highway. That's neuroplasticity."

Andrew Huberman Framework: Neurons that fire together, wire together. Repetition + focused attention = pathway strengthening. The breakthrough wasn't luck—it was evidence that new wiring is forming.

Part 2: Boundaries as Neural Pathway Practice

"Now, here's how boundaries fit in. A boundary is just a decision you make in advance about how you're going to respond to a situation. When you don't have boundaries, your brain defaults to autopilot—the old pathways. When you do have boundaries, you're giving your brain a pre-planned route. You're saying: 'When X happens, I will do Y.' That's how you build new pathways."

"For example: If your dad asks how you're doing and you feel overwhelmed, you might default to 'I'm fine' (old pathway—people-pleasing, shutting down). A boundary would sound like: 'Dad, I'm actually struggling right now, and I need to take a break. Can we talk later?' That's a new pathway. You're practicing honesty and self-care instead of avoidance."

Part 3: Joe Dispenza's 'Thoughts Create Our State of Being'

"Joe Dispenza says that our thoughts literally create our state of being. When you think 'I'm basically a drug addict,' your body responds like that's true—you feel hopeless, your shoulders slump, your energy drops, and cravings feel inevitable. But when you think 'I'm going through something hard, and I'm learning to handle it differently,' your body responds to that—you feel more grounded, more capable, more in control. Same situation. Different thought. Different state. Different outcome."

"Boundaries work the same way. When you think 'I can't say no to my parents,' you create a state of powerlessness. When you think 'I love my parents and I get to take care of myself,' you create a state of empowerment. That's what we're practicing today."

Check for Understanding: "Does this make sense? What questions come up for you about how this applies to your situation right now?"
```

Boundary Setting Practice: Parents & Living Situation

```

Understanding the Green-Yellow-Red Boundary Method

"Before we dive into specific scenarios, I want to teach you a simple framework for setting boundaries called the Green-Yellow-Red method. This comes from Melissa Urban, and it's going to make boundary-setting way less overwhelming."

The Green-Yellow-Red System:
  • Green Boundaries: These are your proactive, everyday boundaries. They're gentle and polite—your "no big deal" boundaries that you set right from the start.
  • Yellow Boundaries: If someone pushes past your green boundary, yellow is a firmer, clearer reminder. It's you saying, "I really mean this, and I'm sticking to it."
  • Red Boundaries: This is your hard stop. If someone still doesn't respect your boundary after you've communicated it once or twice, red is non-negotiable. It's not about being mean—it's about protecting your well-being. You're stating a consequence: "Here's what I'm going to do now."

Examples: How This Works in Real Life

Private Life Example:

  • Green: "I'm keeping my evenings tech-free after 9 PM."
  • Yellow: "I'm not available to chat after 9, let's catch up tomorrow."
  • Red: "If I keep getting late-night calls, I'll have to put my phone on do-not-disturb and we can chat the next day."

Home/Family Life Example:

  • Green: "I need an hour of quiet time in the evening to wind down."
  • Yellow: "I really need this quiet hour to recharge, so I'll be in my room for a bit."
  • Red: "If I can't get this quiet time at home, I'll have to step out for a walk or find a quiet space elsewhere."

Work Life Example:

  • Green: "I'm not taking work calls after 6 PM."
  • Yellow: "I'm off the clock now and I'll handle this first thing tomorrow."
  • Red: "If work calls keep coming in after hours, I'll have to turn off notifications and respond during business hours only."

Identifying Your Boundary Needs

"Now let's get specific about where you need boundaries. I'm going to ask you some questions, and I want you to notice what comes up—both in your thoughts and in your body."

Boundary Exploration Questions:
  • In your private life: Where do you feel like your time or energy is getting drained? Is there a part of your day where you need more personal space?
  • In your family life: Are there situations at home where you feel like you're overextending yourself or not being heard? What's one boundary that would help you feel more balanced?
  • What does your dad do or say that feels supportive? What does he do or say that feels like too much?
  • Your mom is visiting right now. What do you need from her during this visit? What would feel intrusive?
  • When your family asks about your recovery or how you're doing, what feels helpful? What feels like pressure?
  • If you could design the perfect amount of check-ins from your dad—how often, what format, what content—what would that look like?
  • What do you need to feel like you have privacy and autonomy while you're staying at his house?
  • What's one thing you've wanted to say to your dad or mom but haven't yet? What stops you?
  • In your work or social life: When you're meeting new people or dealing with work, where do you need to draw the line to keep your recovery on track?

DBT Skill: DEAR MAN (Interpersonal Effectiveness)

"Now let's combine the Green-Yellow-Red system with a DBT tool called DEAR MAN. This gives you a script you can use when you need to ask for something or say no."

  • Describe: State the facts without judgment. "Dad, I really appreciate you letting me stay here."
  • Express: Share your feelings using "I" statements. "I'm feeling a bit overwhelmed."
  • Assert: Ask clearly for what you need. "I need some space in the evenings to decompress alone."
  • Reinforce: Explain the positive outcome. "It'll help me stay grounded and work on my recovery."
  • Mindful: Stay focused on your goal—don't get sidetracked or defensive.
  • Appear confident: Even if you're nervous, use a calm, steady tone.
  • Negotiate: Be willing to compromise. "What would work for you?"

Practice Scenario #1: Setting a Boundary with Dad

"Let's say your dad has been checking in on you a lot—maybe more than you need right now. Let's create your Green-Yellow-Red boundary and then use DEAR MAN to practice how you'd communicate the green level."

Interactive Practice:

Step 1: Create your three levels:

  • Green: (Your everyday boundary with dad)
  • Yellow: (Firmer reminder if needed)
  • Red: (Your non-negotiable consequence)

Step 2: Use DEAR MAN to practice communicating your green boundary.

Practice Scenario #2: Setting a Boundary with Mom

"Your mom is visiting from Regina. Let's say she asks a lot of questions about your recovery, and it starts to feel like too much. How would you let her know you need a break from that topic while still appreciating her care? Let's build out your Green-Yellow-Red levels for this situation."

Interactive Practice:

Work through the three boundary levels and practice the green boundary using DEAR MAN.

Session Note: Emphasize that boundaries are acts of love—they protect relationships by preventing resentment. They're not rejection; they're clarity. Remind that boundaries are a form of self-respect and help show up as the best version of yourself.

Planning for Downtown Transition

"You said this is a stepping stone, and eventually you want to move back downtown—somewhere walkable, with more people your age, more events, coffee shops, pizza places. Let's talk about what needs to happen before you're ready for that."

Future-Focused Questions:
  • "What would tell you that you're ready to live alone again?"
  • "What does 'strong in sobriety' look like to you? What markers would show you're there?"
  • "You mentioned Oliver's residences—what appeals to you about that kind of community?"
  • "If you moved downtown in 3-6 months, what supports would you need to have in place?"
  • "What would you want to be different about your relationship with yourself before you make that move?"
Reframe: "You're not stuck in the suburbs. You're strategically building a foundation. When you move downtown, you'll be doing it from a place of strength, not escape. That's a completely different move. Use these boundaries to create a life where your recovery and your autonomy go hand in hand."
```

Strengthening Neural Pathways: Thought Replacement Practice

```

Identifying Old vs. New Pathways

"Let's map out some of your old neural pathways and create replacement thoughts—new pathways—that you're going to practice this week. We'll use your breakthrough from yesterday as the template. Let's work through this together."

Old Pathway → New Pathway Exercise:
Old Pathway (Addicted Self) New Pathway (Sober Self)
"I'm basically a drug addict" "I'm someone who struggles with addictive thinking, and I'm rewiring my brain"
"I can't trust myself" "I'm learning to trust myself one decision at a time"
"I need my dad here or I'll relapse" "My dad is helping me build a foundation so I can be strong on my own"
"I'm stuck in the suburbs and isolated" "This is a stepping stone—I'm preparing to thrive downtown when I'm ready"

Daily Thought Replacement Practice

"Here's your homework: Every time you catch an old pathway thought, I want you to pause, say it out loud or write it down, and then replace it with the new pathway version. Do this out loud if you can—speaking activates different neural circuits than just thinking. The more you practice, the faster the new pathway builds."

Neuroplasticity Reminder: "Remember, this isn't about positive thinking or lying to yourself. It's about accuracy. The old thoughts are distortions. The new thoughts are actually more true. You're not a drug addict—you're someone in recovery. You're not stuck—you're strategically building strength. That's not spin. That's reality."
```
